2.1 - Introduction to Rational Expressions & Restrictions

Rational expressions are typically the quotient of two polynomials. Examples of rational expressions are: 2+x/3+x or x-2/x2+9



Restrictions in rational expressions are numbers that the variable cannot be equal to or the expression may result in being an undefined value. To find restrictions, we would take the denominator of the expression and convert it into an express which does not equal to zero.
For example, the equation x+3/x-3:
x-3≠0
x≠3
So from this process we can are able to see that in this expression, x cannot be equal to 3 or else it would be an undefined value.

2.2 - Simplifying Rational Expressions

Since rational expressions are typically the quotient of two polynomials, you would have to take what we have learned during unit 1 and simplify the polynomials (if possible) in the quotients. Please pay careful attention to this specific part of the unit as the ability to simplify rational functions is a crucial component. Mistakes in simplifying rational functions will result in errors when solving for rational functions/expressions.


In order to simplify a rational function, we must first look at the polynomial at its numerator and then we can simplify the polynomial in the denominator. Keep in mind factoring is a form of simplifying an equation so we may use what we have learned from unit 1. Lets take a quick look at x2+9x+18/x2+6x



For the equation x2+9x+18/x2+6x, we may first take a look at the numerator (x2+9x+18) and see how we may want to simplify/factor it. In this case, x2+9x+18 is a trinomial allowing us to use the methods we have learned in unit 1. This would result in the numerator turning from x2+9x+18 to (x+6)(x-4)



Example: x2+9x+18/x2+6x

This is a common example of a rational function that you will encouter throughout your entire life when learning pre-calculus/calculus. With different types of polynomial in the numerator and denominator, there is no standard rule to solving rational functions. You must learn the ability to access each rational function and use all your prior knowledge in simplification/factorization.

Take a look at the numerator (x2+9x+18)

We must ask ourselves the question, "how can we factor/simplify the numerator: x2+9x+18?"

  • We recognized that the numerator is a simple trinomial
  • It can be factored using the method of (x + a)(x + b) that we have learned during unit 1.1

So x2+9x+18 would turn into (x + 3)(x + 6) because 3 + 6 adds up to 9 and 3 x 6 multiplies to 18.

Take a look at the denominator (x2+6x)

This polynomial is a bit different as it is a binomial. We aren't able to factor it using unit 1 as it is not a trinomial. We will just have to general factor it!

x2+6x may appear like we cannot general factor it but in fact, we actually can! We can convert x2+6x to x(x + 6) since x is in both of the terms.

Combine the two factorization and see if you can cancel any terms out (x + 3)(x + 6)/x(x + 6)

Looking at the equation, we can cancel the (x + 6) since it appears on both the denominator and numerator. Keep in mind, you can only cancel the terms if the numerator and denominator is factorized. Otherwise, it would be wrong.

Check the final answer (x+3)/x

Make sure to go over and double check if there were any mistakes. If not, the final answer should be (x+3)/x

2.3 - Multiplication and Division of Rational Expressions

Dealing with rational expressions with multiplication and division may be a bit more harder than the previous units but it is generally the same concept.


First, we must realize that the multiplication and division of rational expressions is simply just multiplication and division with fractions.


Multiplication Example: x2-25/x2-4 · 4x-8/3x-15

When multiplying fractions, we may just multiply the denominator with the other fraction's denominator and apply the same rules to the numerator. As simple as it sounds, we will also have to simplify the product in order to reach the final answer.

Multiplying the entire expression

x2-25/x2-4 · 4x-8/3x-15 multiplied together just becomes (x-5)(x+5)(4)(x-2)/(x-2)(x+2)(3)(x-5)

As tempting as it is to FOIL this equation to expand it, we have to cancel out any like terms since it is already factored. Expanding it will only cause us to have an extra step in factoring the equation.

Simplifying (x-5)(x+5)(4)(x-2)/(x-2)(x+2)(3)(x-5)

(x-5)(x+5)(4)(x-2)/(x-2)(x+2)(3)(x-5) will turn into 4(x+5)/3(x+2) since all the other terms can be cancelled out.

Check the final answer

Make sure to go over and double check as a lot of common mistakes are usually found in the simplification process. The final answer should be 4(x+5)/3(x+2)

2.4 - Addition and Subtraction of Rational Expressions

Addition and Subtraction essentially follows the same rules. We need to have a common denominator between the two rational functions we want to add/subtract. The ability to find the common denominator is a major skill and actually requires quite a lot of practice. If you get the wrong common denominator when calculating, it could result in the whole equation being an error.


Finding Common Denominator


Finding the common denominator is a bit more complicated with rational functions. Here is a video from GreeneMath.

Finding Common Denominator Example: 3x/3x+6 + 1/x+2

Find the Denominator

In this case, the two denominators are 3x+6 and x+2.

Simplify the each term's denominator and try to find the common denominator

3x+6 will turn into 3(x+2) and x+2 is already simplified. So we can tell that the common denominator is 3(x+2).

Convert the Fractions into common denominators.

3x/3x+6 will turn into 3x/3(x+2) since we can just factor the denominator. As for 1/x+2, we need to multiply the entire equation by 3 in order to get the common denominator. This would result in 3(1/x+2) which turns to 3/3(x+2)

Add the two expressions

The final expression will turn into 3x/3(x+2) + 3/3(x+2)

3x/3(x+2) + 3/3(x+2)

3x+3/3(x+2)

3(x+1)/3(x+2)

(x+1)/(x+2)
